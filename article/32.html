Perturbing Developments

Last time, we finished our introductory analysis of the propagator. It implied that physics looks fairly similar forwards and backwards in time, and that probabilities are mostly (but not entirely!) contributed by processes that obey the energy-momentum identity.

Today, we will start to study the other part of the path integral: the vacuum perturbations. We will do some nice tricks to coerce the integral to momentum space, then analyze how two different perturbations might interact. Our analysis will lead us towards the discovery of forces.

Analyzing the Path Exponent

Recall the path integral for a particle moving from point x1x_1x1​ to point x2x_2x2​:

∫ei2∫∫J(x2)†D−1(x2−x1)J(x1)d4x1d4x2dy(t)\int e^{\frac{i}{2} \int \int J(x_2)^\dag D^{-1}(x_2 - x_1) J(x_1) d^4 x_1 d^4 x_2} dy(t)∫e2i​∫∫J(x2​)†D−1(x2​−x1​)J(x1​)d4x1​d4x2​dy(t)

If it helps you to remember, this double integral is the continuous version of a matrix-vector product.

All the interesting stuff is happening in the exponent’s integral. Thus we will consider this integral by itself:

∫∫J(x2)†D−1(x2−x1)J(x1)d4x1d4x2\int \int J(x_2)^\dag D^{-1}(x_2 - x_1) J(x_1) d^4 x_1 d^4 x_2∫∫J(x2​)†D−1(x2​−x1​)J(x1​)d4x1​d4x2​

We can now substitute in the momentum expression we found for the propagator:

D−1(x2−x1)=1(2π)4∫ei(x2−x1)pp2−m2+iϵd4pD^{-1}(x_2 - x_1) = \frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int \frac{e^{i (x_2 - x_1) p}}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} d^4 pD−1(x2​−x1​)=(2π)41​∫p2−m2+iϵei(x2​−x1​)p​d4p

This gives us

1(2π)4∫∫J(x2)†(∫ei(x2−x1)pp2−m2+iϵd4p)J(x1)d4x1d4x2\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int \int J(x_2)^\dag (\int \frac{e^{i (x_2 - x_1) p}}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} d^4 p) J(x_1) d^4 x_1 d^4 x_2(2π)41​∫∫J(x2​)†(∫p2−m2+iϵei(x2​−x1​)p​d4p)J(x1​)d4x1​d4x2​

Since the perturbation functions are constant with respect to ppp, we can pull them into the momentum integral:

1(2π)4∫∫∫J(x2)†ei(x2−x1)pp2−m2+iϵJ(x1)d4pd4x1d4x2\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int \int \int J(x_2)^\dag \frac{e^{i (x_2 - x_1) p}}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} J(x_1) d^4 p d^4 x_1 d^4 x_2(2π)41​∫∫∫J(x2​)†p2−m2+iϵei(x2​−x1​)p​J(x1​)d4pd4x1​d4x2​

One might reasonably be concerned that we’ve just made everything worse. We’ve gone from two integrals to three – how could this be better?

The answer is: with some help from exponent properties and factorization.

Into Momentum Space

Let’s look a bit closer at this path exponent integral:

1(2π)4∫∫∫J(x2)†ei(x2−x1)pp2−m2+iϵJ(x1)d4pd4x1d4x2\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int \int \int J(x_2)^\dag \frac{e^{i (x_2 - x_1) p}}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} J(x_1) d^4 p d^4 x_1 d^4 x_2(2π)41​∫∫∫J(x2​)†p2−m2+iϵei(x2​−x1​)p​J(x1​)d4pd4x1​d4x2​

Notice that x2x_2x2​ and x1x_1x1​ are only flimsily connected. In fact, we can seperate them entirely using exponent properties:

1(2π)4∫∫∫J(x2)†ei(x2−x1)pp2−m2+iϵJ(x1)d4pd4x1d4x2=1(2π)4∫∫∫J(x2)†eix2pe−ix1pp2−m2+iϵJ(x1)d4pd4x1d4x2\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int \int \int J(x_2)^\dag \frac{e^{i (x_2 - x_1) p}}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} J(x_1) d^4 p d^4 x_1 d^4 x_2 = \frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int \int \int J(x_2)^\dag \frac{e^{i x_2 p} e^{-i x_1 p}}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} J(x_1) d^4 p d^4 x_1 d^4 x_2(2π)41​∫∫∫J(x2​)†p2−m2+iϵei(x2​−x1​)p​J(x1​)d4pd4x1​d4x2​=(2π)41​∫∫∫J(x2​)†p2−m2+iϵeix2​pe−ix1​p​J(x1​)d4pd4x1​d4x2​

We might then group the x1x_1x1​ terms together and the x2x_2x2​ terms together:

1(2π)4∫∫∫J(x2)†eix2peix1pp2−m2+iϵJ(x1)d4pd4x1d4x2=1(2π)4∫∫∫e−ix2pJ(x2)†1p2−m2+iϵe−ix1pJ(x1)d4pd4x1d4x2\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int \int \int J(x_2)^\dag \frac{e^{i x_2 p} e^{i x_1 p}}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} J(x_1) d^4 p d^4 x_1 d^4 x_2 = \frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int \int \int e^{-i x_2 p} J(x_2)^\dag \frac{1}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} e^{-i x_1 p} J(x_1) d^4 p d^4 x_1 d^4 x_2(2π)41​∫∫∫J(x2​)†p2−m2+iϵeix2​peix1​p​J(x1​)d4pd4x1​d4x2​=(2π)41​∫∫∫e−ix2​pJ(x2​)†p2−m2+iϵ1​e−ix1​pJ(x1​)d4pd4x1​d4x2​

Hold on. Not only are the x1x_1x1​ and x2x_2x2​ terms full seperate, they’re both integrated over… which means that all of a sudden, we can factorize this integral!

1(2π)4∫∫∫eix2pJ(x2)†1p2−m2+iϵeix1pJ(x1)d4pd4x1d4x2=1(2π)4∫1p2−m2+iϵ(∫eix2pJ(x2)†d4x2)(∫e−ix1pJ(x1)d4x1)dp\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int \int \int e^{i x_2 p} J(x_2)^\dag \frac{1}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} e^{i x_1 p} J(x_1) d^4 p d^4 x_1 d^4 x_2 = \frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int \frac{1}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} (\int e^{i x_2 p} J(x_2)^\dag d^4 x_2) (\int e^{-i x_1 p} J(x_1) d^4 x_1) dp(2π)41​∫∫∫eix2​pJ(x2​)†p2−m2+iϵ1​eix1​pJ(x1​)d4pd4x1​d4x2​=(2π)41​∫p2−m2+iϵ1​(∫eix2​pJ(x2​)†d4x2​)(∫e−ix1​pJ(x1​)d4x1​)dp

Lo and behold, these position integrals are nothing more than the Fourier transforms (i.e., the momentum expressions) of the perturbation J(x)J(x)J(x)!

Well, almost. We do need to be a little careful with the x2x_2x2​ integral, since the sign in the exponent isn’t quite right. This problem is solved by realizing that this should be the complex conjugate of the Fourier transform of J(x2)J(x_2)J(x2​):

JP(p)†=(∫e−ipxJ(x)d4x)†=∫eipxJ(x)dagd4xJ_P(p)^\dag = (\int e^{-i p x} J(x) d^4 x)^\dag = \int e^{i p x} J(x)^ dag d^4 xJP​(p)†=(∫e−ipxJ(x)d4x)†=∫eipxJ(x)dagd4x

Thus the above integrals are just the Fourier transforms. We can then write the path exponent in a surprisingly simple form:

1(2π)4∫1p2−m2+iϵ(∫eix2pJ(x2)†d4x2)(∫e−ix1pJ(x1)d4x1)dp=1(2π)4∫J(p)†1p2−m2+iϵJ(p)dp\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int \frac{1}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} (\int e^{i x_2 p} J(x_2)^\dag d^4 x_2) (\int e^{-i x_1 p} J(x_1) d^4 x_1) dp = \frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int J(p)^\dag \frac{1}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} J(p) dp(2π)41​∫p2−m2+iϵ1​(∫eix2​pJ(x2​)†d4x2​)(∫e−ix1​pJ(x1​)d4x1​)dp=(2π)41​∫J(p)†p2−m2+iϵ1​J(p)dp

Note that we are omitting the PPP subscript for a function in the momentum basis. This is to allow us to attach more subscripts later. We will preserve the information about basis using the variable (e.g. xxx vs. ppp). From now on, we will indicate all bases in this way.

We now see that the propagator and the perturbations seem almost made for each other. The exponent in the propagator plays beautifully with the two perturbation terms at different positions, resulting in an integral that’s far cleaner than anything we’ve seen in a while.

Interaction Terms

Let’s play a little with our new expression. Let’s assume that the vacuum perturbation J(p)J(p)J(p) is simply the sum of two small perturbations:

J(p)=J1(p)+J2(p)J(p) = J_1(p) + J_2(p)J(p)=J1​(p)+J2​(p)

The path exponent we just derived then becomes

1(2π)4∫(J1(p)+J2(p))†(J1(p)+J2(p))1p2−m2+iϵd4p\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int (J_1(p) + J_2(p))^\dag (J_1(p) + J_2(p)) \frac{1}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon}d^4 p(2π)41​∫(J1​(p)+J2​(p))†(J1​(p)+J2​(p))p2−m2+iϵ1​d4p

The leading term factors into four smaller terms. Two of these will be terms with just J1J_1J1​ or J2J_2J2​, which we have already seen. However, we also find some terms in which the two disturbances J1J_1J1​ and J2J_2J2​ seem to interact:

1(2π)4∫J2(p)†1p2−m2+iϵJ1(p)d4p\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^4} \int J_2(p)^\dag \frac{1}{p^2 - m^2 + i \epsilon} J_1(p) d^4 p(2π)41​∫J2​(p)†p2−m2+iϵ1​J1​(p)d4p

Note that the conjugate term also exists.

This is very interesting. It seems like if two perturbations J1,J2J_1, J_2J1​,J2​ have overlapping momentum expressions, then they can affect how a particle moves. Furthermore, as before, they will have the most power if they overlap near the “rule-following” value p2−m2p^2 - m^2p2−m2. Thus we reach a very interesting conclusion:

Two field perturbations that are distant in space can affect the motion of a particle, so long as their Fourier transforms overlap at values near the momentum of that particle.

Tomorrow, we will see how this simple-seeing expression gives rise to the forces of nature – all from working with linear vacuum perturbations.
