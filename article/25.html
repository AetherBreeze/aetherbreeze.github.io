The Gaussian Integral (Now with Matrices!)

Last time, we temporarily discretized spacetime in order to get rid of the derivatives in the path integral. Now we will justify that nonsense by evaluating the resulting integral.

Factorizing the Integral

Recall the discretized path integral, which we’ve written in terms of the discretized field vector ∣F⟩\ket{F}∣F⟩:

(Πa=1N∫−∞∞dFa)eiℏ∑−12⟨F∣(D2+m2)∣F⟩+⟨J∣∣F⟩\left(\Pi_{a=1}^{N} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dF_a\right) e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} \sum -\frac{1}{2} \bra{F} (D^2 + m^2) \ket{F} + \bra{J} \ket{F}}(Πa=1N​∫−∞∞​dFa​)eℏi​∑−21​⟨F∣(D2+m2)∣F⟩+⟨J∣∣F⟩

A good wa to approach these kinds of equations is to look at the scariest part first and try to make it less scary. Right now, the scariest part is definitely the product of NNN integrals. Our lives would be much eaiser if we could consider each of these integrals individually. This would mean integrating over one element FaF_aFa​ of the field vector at a time.

Unfortunately, we can’t currently factor the integrals. The complicated term is the matrix one, ⟨F∣D2∣F⟩\bra{F} D^2 \ket{F}⟨F∣D2∣F⟩. This term blends the elements of ∣F⟩\ket{F}∣F⟩ in a complicated way.

What would be really nice is if D2D^2D2 was diagonal. In that case, we could reduce D2∣F⟩D^2 \ket{F}D2∣F⟩ to da2Fa∣Fa⟩d_a^2 F_a \ket{F_a}da2​Fa​∣Fa​⟩, where dad_ada​ is the aaath eigenvalue of DDD. We could then use exponential properites to factor the exponential into one for each FaF_aFa​, at which point we could use our old Gaussian integral result.

Unfortunately, in general, the derivative matrix DDD is not diagonal… yet.

Diagonal Derivative Coordinates

Once we identify that DDD being diagonal would make things easier, our thoughts should immediately jump to finding a coordinate system in which it is. This is quite simple. We can diagonalize DDD into some eigenvalue matrix DED_EDE​ and some orthogonal rotation matrix RRR:

D=R−1DERD = R^{-1} D_E RD=R−1DE​R

The rotation matrices cancel out of the square:

D2=R−1DE2RD^2 = R^{-1} D_E^2 RD2=R−1DE2​R

Substituting this into our integral, and seperating the troublesome matrix term, we get

(Πa=1N∫−∞∞dFa)eiℏ∑−12⟨F∣R−1DE2R∣F⟩−m22⟨F∣∣F⟩+⟨J∣∣F⟩\left(\Pi_{a=1}^{N} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dF_a\right) e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} \sum -\frac{1}{2} \bra{F} R^{-1} D_E^2 R \ket{F} - \frac{m^2}{2} \bra{F} \ket{F} + \bra{J} \ket{F}}(Πa=1N​∫−∞∞​dFa​)eℏi​∑−21​⟨F∣R−1DE2​R∣F⟩−2m2​⟨F∣∣F⟩+⟨J∣∣F⟩

We can now invoke a clever change of coordinates. Note that since RRR is an orthogonal matrix, we have R†=R−1R^\dag = R^{-1}R†=R−1. Thus the matrix term above is just

⟨F∣R−1DE2R∣F⟩=(R∣F⟩)†DE2(R∣F⟩)\bra{F} R^{-1} D_E^2 R \ket{F} = (R \ket{F})^\dag D_E^2 (R \ket{F})⟨F∣R−1DE2​R∣F⟩=(R∣F⟩)†DE2​(R∣F⟩)

This immediately suggests we swap to the coordinates

∣G⟩=R∣F⟩\ket{G} = R \ket{F}∣G⟩=R∣F⟩

These are called the diagonal derivative coordinates, for the complex theoretical reason that they make the derivative matrix diagonal. They are extremely nice because they don’t change the integration factors dFadF_adFa​ (since the change of coordinates matrix has determinant 1). Swapping to them simplifies the derivative term greatly:

(Πa=1N∫−∞∞dGa)eiℏ∑−12⟨G∣DE2∣G⟩−m22⟨G∣∣G⟩+⟨J∣R−1∣G⟩\left(\Pi_{a=1}^{N} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dG_a\right) e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} \sum -\frac{1}{2} \bra{G} D_E^2 \ket{G} - \frac{m^2}{2} \bra{G} \ket{G} + \bra{J} R^{-1} \ket{G}}(Πa=1N​∫−∞∞​dGa​)eℏi​∑−21​⟨G∣DE2​∣G⟩−2m2​⟨G∣∣G⟩+⟨J∣R−1∣G⟩

Success! The first term now contains a diagonal matrix and can be factored nicely.

There is one minor complication. In swapping to diagonal derivative coordinates, we have inadvertently messed up the vacuum perturbation term:

⟨J∣∣F⟩=⟨J∣R−1∣G⟩\bra{J} \ket{F} = \bra{J} R^{-1} \ket{G}⟨J∣∣F⟩=⟨J∣R−1∣G⟩

This is a good exercise. Think now. How can we make this term nice for GGG again using tools we’ve already seen this section?

For those of you who recalled the relation R−1=R†R^{-1} = R^\dagR−1=R† – very good! This key property of rotation matrices lets us group the troublesome matrix RRR with ⟨J∣\bra{J}⟨J∣ instead of our coordinate vector ∣G⟩\ket{G}∣G⟩:

⟨J∣R−1∣G⟩=(R∣J⟩)†∣G⟩\bra{J} R^{-1} \ket{G} = (R \ket{J})^\dag \ket{G}⟨J∣R−1∣G⟩=(R∣J⟩)†∣G⟩

Factoring The Integral

After swapping to diagonal derivative coordinates, the above integral enters the relatively simple form:

(Πa=1N∫−∞∞dGa)eiℏ∑−12⟨G∣DE2∣G⟩−m22⟨G∣∣G⟩+(R∣J⟩)†∣G⟩\left(\Pi_{a=1}^{N} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dG_a\right) e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} \sum -\frac{1}{2} \bra{G} D_E^2 \ket{G} - \frac{m^2}{2} \bra{G} \ket{G} + (R \ket{J})^\dag \ket{G}}(Πa=1N​∫−∞∞​dGa​)eℏi​∑−21​⟨G∣DE2​∣G⟩−2m2​⟨G∣∣G⟩+(R∣J⟩)†∣G⟩

This may not look any simpler at a glance. However, since every term is now a multiple of ∣G⟩\ket{G}∣G⟩ or a diagonal matrix acting on ∣G⟩\ket{G}∣G⟩, we can now perform the crucial step of splitting this exponent into terms proportional to the elements GaG_aGa​:

(Πa=1N∫−∞∞dGa)eiℏ∑∑b=1N−12[DE]b2Gb2−m22Gb2+(R∣J⟩)b†Gb\left(\Pi_{a=1}^{N} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dG_a\right) e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} \sum \sum_{b=1}^{N} -\frac{1}{2} [D_E]_b^2 G_b^2 - \frac{m^2}{2} G_b^2 + (R \ket{J})_b^\dag G_b}(Πa=1N​∫−∞∞​dGa​)eℏi​∑∑b=1N​−21​[DE​]b2​Gb2​−2m2​Gb2​+(R∣J⟩)b†​Gb​

where [DE]b[D_E]_b[DE​]b​ is the bbbth eigenvalue of the diagonalized derivative matrix DDD. Grouping like terms, this becomes

(Πa=1N∫−∞∞dGa)eiℏ∑∑b=1N−12([DE]b2+m2)Gb2+(R∣J⟩)b†Gb\left(\Pi_{a=1}^{N} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dG_a\right) e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} \sum \sum_{b=1}^{N} -\frac{1}{2} ([D_E]_b^2 + m^2) G_b^2 + (R \ket{J})_b^\dag G_b}(Πa=1N​∫−∞∞​dGa​)eℏi​∑∑b=1N​−21​([DE​]b2​+m2)Gb2​+(R∣J⟩)b†​Gb​

We can now use exponential properties to factor this into NNN exponentials, each of whose exponent is only a function of the single variable GbG_bGb​:

(Πa=1N∫−∞∞dGa)Πb=1Neiℏ−12([DE]b2+m2)Gb2+(R∣J⟩)b†Gb\left(\Pi_{a=1}^{N} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dG_a\right) \Pi_{b=1}^{N} e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} -\frac{1}{2} ([D_E]_b^2 + m^2) G_b^2 + (R \ket{J})_b^\dag G_b}(Πa=1N​∫−∞∞​dGa​)Πb=1N​eℏi​−21​([DE​]b2​+m2)Gb2​+(R∣J⟩)b†​Gb​

Finally, the moment we’ve been waiting for: we can factor this into a product of integrals over single variables:

Πa=1N∫−∞∞eiℏ(−12([DE]a2+m2)Ga2+(R∣J⟩)a†Ga)dGa\Pi_{a=1}^{N} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} (-\frac{1}{2} ([D_E]_a^2 + m^2) G_a^2 + (R \ket{J})_a^\dag G_a)} dG_aΠa=1N​∫−∞∞​eℏi​(−21​([DE​]a2​+m2)Ga2​+(R∣J⟩)a†​Ga​)dGa​

Finally, We Can Be Lazy

The moment has finally come. Every element of this product is just a Gaussian integral. We know the solution to this integral:

∫−∞∞e−cx2+dxdx=ed24cπc\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-c x^2 + d x} dx = e^{\frac{d^2}{4 c}} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{c}}∫−∞∞​e−cx2+dxdx=e4cd2​cπ​​

It’s elementary school math time. Plugging:

c=i([DE]a2+m2)2ℏc = \frac{i ([D_E]_a^2 + m^2)}{2 \hbar}c=2ℏi([DE​]a2​+m2)​
d=i(R∣J⟩)aℏd = \frac{i (R \ket{J})_a}{\hbar}d=ℏi(R∣J⟩)a​​

and chugging:

d24c=−(R∣J⟩)a2ℏ2ℏ2i([DE]a2+m2)=i(R∣J⟩)a22ℏ([DE]a2+m2)\frac{d^2}{4 c} = -\frac{(R \ket{J})_a^2}{\hbar^2} \frac{\hbar}{2 i ([D_E]_a^2 + m^2)} = \frac{i (R \ket{J})_a^2}{2 \hbar ([D_E]_a^2 + m^2)}4cd2​=−ℏ2(R∣J⟩)a2​​2i([DE​]a2​+m2)ℏ​=2ℏ([DE​]a2​+m2)i(R∣J⟩)a2​​
∫−∞∞eiℏ(−12([DE]a2+m2)Ga2+(R∣J⟩)a†Ga)dGa=ei(R∣J⟩)a22ℏ([DE]a2+m2)2πℏi([DE]a2+m2)\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} (-\frac{1}{2} ([D_E]_a^2 + m^2) G_a^2 + (R \ket{J})_a^\dag G_a)} dG_a = e^{\frac{i (R \ket{J})_a^2}{2 \hbar ([D_E]_a^2 + m^2)}} \sqrt{\frac{2 \pi \hbar}{i ([D_E]_a^2 + m^2)}}∫−∞∞​eℏi​(−21​([DE​]a2​+m2)Ga2​+(R∣J⟩)a†​Ga​)dGa​=e2ℏ([DE​]a2​+m2)i(R∣J⟩)a2​​i([DE​]a2​+m2)2πℏ​​

Finally, we take a product over all discrete field values FaF_aFa​ to get our discretized path integral:

Πa=1N∫−∞∞eiℏ(−12([DE]a2+m2)Ga2+(R∣J⟩)a†Ga)dGa=Πa=1Nei(R∣J⟩)a22ℏ([DE]a2+m2)2πℏi([DE]a2+m2)\Pi_{a=1}^{N} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} (-\frac{1}{2} ([D_E]_a^2 + m^2) G_a^2 + (R \ket{J})_a^\dag G_a)} dG_a = \Pi_{a=1}^{N} e^{\frac{i (R \ket{J})_a^2}{2 \hbar ([D_E]_a^2 + m^2)}} \sqrt{\frac{2 \pi \hbar}{i ([D_E]_a^2 + m^2)}}Πa=1N​∫−∞∞​eℏi​(−21​([DE​]a2​+m2)Ga2​+(R∣J⟩)a†​Ga​)dGa​=Πa=1N​e2ℏ([DE​]a2​+m2)i(R∣J⟩)a2​​i([DE​]a2​+m2)2πℏ​​

Back To Vectors

In general, expressions in terms of vector and matrix components (like the above) are nicer to work with when they’re in terms of general vectors and matrices. Thus we will try to put it back in that form.

The square root term is the easy one. Consider the denominator of this term (sans the iii):

Πa=1N[DE]a2+m2\Pi_{a=1}^N [D_E]_a^2 + m^2Πa=1N​[DE​]a2​+m2

This is almost the product of the eigenvalues of the derivative matrix. If it was, we could recognize this as simply the determinant of the derivative matrix, ∣D∣|D|∣D∣. We can make this leap by introducing the mass-shifted derivative matrix Dˉ\bar{D}Dˉ:

Dˉ:=D2+m2I\bar{D} := D^2 + m^2 IDˉ:=D2+m2I

Is this cheating, since this DDD technically isn’t diagonalized? No, because the mass matrix m2Im^2 Im2I is the same in every basis, but good question.

We can now rewrite the square root term very cleanly in terms of the mass-shifted derivative matrix’s determinant:

πa=1N2πℏi([DE]a2+m2)=(−2iπℏ)N∣Dˉ∣\pi_{a=1}^{N} \sqrt{\frac{2 \pi \hbar}{i ([D_E]_a^2 + m^2)}} = \sqrt{\frac{(-2 i \pi \hbar)^N}{|\bar{D}|}}πa=1N​i([DE​]a2​+m2)2πℏ​​=∣Dˉ∣(−2iπℏ)N​​

where we have swapped the iii in the denominator for a −i-i−i in the numerator.

Now let’s consider the tricky part:

πa=1Nei(R∣J⟩)a22ℏ([DE]a2+m2)\pi_{a=1}^{N} e^{\frac{i (R \ket{J})_a^2}{2 \hbar ([D_E]_a^2 + m^2)}}πa=1N​e2ℏ([DE​]a2​+m2)i(R∣J⟩)a2​​

The exponent here is some scalar involving the rotated vacuum perturbation R∣J⟩R \ket{J}R∣J⟩ and the mass-shifted derivative matrix Dˉ\bar{D}Dˉ. We want to rewrite this scalar in terms of the original vectors and matrices. How might we do this?

Well, the usual way of combining a vector and a matrix to make a scalar is

⟨J∣Dˉ∣J⟩\bra{J} \bar{D} \ket{J}⟨J∣Dˉ∣J⟩

Could we pull this “dream expression” out of the above expression?

Well, since our dream expression involves a dot product, we’ll need to sum over the values of aaa. Exponential properties come to the rescue; the product outside the exponent becomes a sum inside of it:

ei2ℏ∑a=1N(R∣J⟩)a2([DE]a2+m2)e^{\frac{i}{2 \hbar} \sum_{a=1}^{N} \frac{(R \ket{J})_a^2}{([D_E]_a^2 + m^2)}}e2ℏi​∑a=1N​([DE​]a2​+m2)(R∣J⟩)a2​​

Things are starting to look good. Since the mass-shifted derivative is diagonal, the above sum is actually its matrix product with the vector (R∣J⟩)a(R \ket{J})_a(R∣J⟩)a​. We can then multiply again by (R∣J⟩)a†(R \ket{J})_a^\dag(R∣J⟩)a†​ on the left to get our dream expression:

ei2ℏ(R∣J⟩)†DEˉ−1(R∣J⟩)e^{\frac{i}{2 \hbar} (R \ket{J})^\dag \bar{D_E}^{-1} (R \ket{J})}e2ℏi​(R∣J⟩)†DE​ˉ​−1(R∣J⟩)

Note one very important thing: since the derivative matrix was in the denominator, the matrix here is the inverse of the derivative matrix. This may seem simple now, but remember: we eventually want to take this back to the continuum limit. When we do so, this will be incredibly consequential.

One last thing. Ideally, we want to leave no trace of our coordinate-rotating shenanigans. We achieved this goal in the square root term because the determinant is independent of basis; however, this time, the rotation matrix RRR seems to stubbornly stick around. Thankfully, once again, the fact that RRR is orthogonal comes in clutch:

ei2ℏ(R∣J⟩)†Dˉ−1(R∣J⟩)=ei2ℏ⟨J∣R†DEˉ−1R∣J⟩e^{\frac{i}{2 \hbar} (R \ket{J})^\dag \bar{D}^{-1} (R \ket{J})} = e^{\frac{i}{2 \hbar} \bra{J} R^\dag \bar{D_E}^{-1} R \ket{J}}e2ℏi​(R∣J⟩)†Dˉ−1(R∣J⟩)=e2ℏi​⟨J∣R†DE​ˉ​−1R∣J⟩

We can now collapse the rotation matrices back into the rotated derivative matrix to get the (inverse of the) original:

ei2ℏ(R∣J⟩)†Dˉ−1(R∣J⟩)=ei2ℏ⟨J∣Dˉ−1∣J⟩e^{\frac{i}{2 \hbar} (R \ket{J})^\dag \bar{D}^{-1} (R \ket{J})} = e^{\frac{i}{2 \hbar} \bra{J} \bar{D}^{-1} \ket{J}}e2ℏi​(R∣J⟩)†Dˉ−1(R∣J⟩)=e2ℏi​⟨J∣Dˉ−1∣J⟩

Substituting both solutions into our path integral, we finally get the discrete solution we desire:

(Πa=1N∫−∞∞dFa)eiℏ∑−12⟨F∣(D2+m2)∣F⟩+⟨J∣∣F⟩=(−2iπℏ)N∣Dˉ∣ei2ℏ⟨J∣Dˉ−1∣J⟩\left(\Pi_{a=1}^{N} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dF_a\right) e^{\frac{i}{\hbar} \sum -\frac{1}{2} \bra{F} (D^2 + m^2) \ket{F} + \bra{J} \ket{F}} = \sqrt{\frac{(-2 i \pi \hbar)^N}{|\bar{D}|}} e^{\frac{i}{2 \hbar} \bra{J} \bar{D}^{-1} \ket{J}}(Πa=1N​∫−∞∞​dFa​)eℏi​∑−21​⟨F∣(D2+m2)∣F⟩+⟨J∣∣F⟩=∣Dˉ∣(−2iπℏ)N​​e2ℏi​⟨J∣Dˉ−1∣J⟩
