The Most Important Integral in Quantum Field Theory

Last time, we used the path integral method to split a single complicated calculation up into several smaller calculations. We then took one of those smaller calculations and simplified it until it was just an integral. Today, we will evaluate that integral – the most widely used integral in quantum field theory.

The Full Integral

The integral at hand is as follows:

12π∫eipn22mℏtNeipn(ya−yb)ℏdpn\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int e^{i \frac{p_n^2}{2 m \hbar} \frac{t}{N}} e^{i \frac{p_n (y_a - y_b)}{\hbar}} dp_n2π1​∫ei2mℏpn2​​Nt​eiℏpn​(ya​−yb​)​dpn​

As is the grand tradition of this series, we will attack this problem in parts. We’ll start with the simplest thing that takes the same form, then slowly build on it until we can calculate the entire thing. To whit, we’ll start with the “simplest interesting part” of this integral:

∫e−x2dx\int e^{-x^2} dx∫e−x2dx

We’ll then quickly generalize this to encompass the first term in the integral:

∫e−cx2dx\int e^{-c x^2} dx∫e−cx2dx

Finally, we’ll add a linear term in the exponent:

∫e−cx2+dxdx\int e^{-c x^2 + d x} dx∫e−cx2+dxdx

This is the essential form of the full integral, and will allow us to finally evaluate our “tiny path probability.”

With all that said… into the breach.

The Gaussian Integral

Consider the first step:

∫e−x2dx\int e^{-x^2} dx∫e−x2dx

This is a deceptively complicated integral. In fact, this integral has no algebraic solution – there is no antiderivative of e−x2e^{-x^2}e−x2 that can be written in terms of standard algebraic functions.

Luckily, we don’t need such an antiderivative. In our original expression, this integral is over all possible values of momentum. In this case, that means all possible values of xxx. Thus we are specifically interested in the integral from −∞-\infty−∞ to ∞\infty∞:

∫−∞∞e−x2dx\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx∫−∞∞​e−x2dx

This can be evaluated. However, it takes quite an absurd trick. This is one of the few tricks in this series that I have trouble motivating. I’ll do my best, but know that this is deep mathematical wizardry.

Deep Mathematical Wizardry

We’ll start by imagining how this integral could be easier. Obviously, it would be easier if it had an antiderivative. Thus we might consider how we could alter this integral to get one with an antiderivative. The simplest way would be to multiply it by 2x2 x2x:

∫−∞∞2xe−x2dx\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} 2 x e^{-x^2} dx∫−∞∞​2xe−x2dx

Any first-year calculus student could tell you the antiderivative of 2xe−x22 x e^{-x^2}2xe−x2 is −e−x2-e^{-x^2}−e−x2. Thus if we can somehow wrangle the integral into this form, we can evaluate it easily.

So how could we make our original integral look like this? Well, one way would be to change coordinates (somehow) to some dydydy such that dy=2xdxdy = 2 x dxdy=2xdx. That would give us the crucial factor we need.

There is actually a promising candidate we might already be familiar with: polar coordinates. Polar coordinates are an alternative 2D coordinate system with the area factor

dxdy=rdrdθdx dy = r dr d\thetadxdy=rdrdθ

Note the multiplication by one of the variables. This is exactly what we need. Unfortunately, our integral is one-dimensional, not two-dimensional. Thus we can’t use polar coordinates here…

…or can we?

Wherein We Use Polar Coordinates Here

Once we have the idea to use polar coordinates, we might start trying to turn our one-dimensional integral into a two-dimensional integral. The easiest way to do this is by squaring the integral, then re-naming the other integration variable:

(∫−∞∞e−x2dx)2=∫−∞∞e−x2dx∫−∞∞e−x2dx=∫−∞∞e−x2dx∫−∞∞e−y2dy\left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx\right)^2 = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^2} dy(∫−∞∞​e−x2dx)2=∫−∞∞​e−x2dx∫−∞∞​e−x2dx=∫−∞∞​e−x2dx∫−∞∞​e−y2dy

Since these integrals are over completely different variables, we can merge them. We can then use exponential properties to simplify the integrand:

∫−∞∞e−x2dx∫−∞∞e−y2dy=∫−∞∞∫−∞∞e−(x2+y2)dxdy\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^2} dy = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-(x^2 + y^2)} dx dy∫−∞∞​e−x2dx∫−∞∞​e−y2dy=∫−∞∞​∫−∞∞​e−(x2+y2)dxdy

And like magic, the radius x2+y2x^2 + y^2x2+y2 appears! The stage is now perfectly set for our change to polar coordinates:

∫−∞∞∫−∞∞e−(x2+y2)dxdy=∫0∞∫02πe−r2rdrdθ\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-(x^2 + y^2)} dx dy = \int_{0}^{\infty} \int_{0}^{2 \pi} e^{-r^2} r dr d\theta∫−∞∞​∫−∞∞​e−(x2+y2)dxdy=∫0∞​∫02π​e−r2rdrdθ

Since the inner expression doesn’t depend on θ\thetaθ, the θ\thetaθ integral is trivial:

∫0∞∫02πe−r2rdrdθ=2π∫0∞e−r2rdr\int_{0}^{\infty} \int_{0}^{2 \pi} e^{-r^2} r dr d\theta = 2 \pi \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-r^2} r dr∫0∞​∫02π​e−r2rdrdθ=2π∫0∞​e−r2rdr

Pulling the factor of two back into the integral and rearranging:

∫0∞∫02πe−r2rdrdθ=π∫0∞2re−r2dr\int_{0}^{\infty} \int_{0}^{2 \pi} e^{-r^2} r dr d\theta = \pi \int_{0}^{\infty} 2 r e^{-r^2} dr∫0∞​∫02π​e−r2rdrdθ=π∫0∞​2re−r2dr

This is the integral we dreamed about earlier. We can evaluate it using its antiderivative:

π∫0∞2re−r2dr=π[−e−r2]0∞=π\pi \int_{0}^{\infty} 2 r e^{-r^2} dr = \pi \left[-e^{-r^2}\right]_0^{\infty} = \piπ∫0∞​2re−r2dr=π[−e−r2]0∞​=π

The number of places that π\piπ shows up out of nowhere is truly absurd.

Thus by hopping up one dimension, we can use polar coordinates to evaluate this integral and find

(∫−∞∞e−x2dx)2=π\left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx\right)^2 = \pi(∫−∞∞​e−x2dx)2=π

and thus

∫−∞∞e−x2dx=π\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx = \sqrt{\pi}∫−∞∞​e−x2dx=π​

Adding In a Constant

We’ve completed the first step of our process. Now we’ll take the second step by adding a constant to the exponent:

∫−∞∞e−cx2dx\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-c x^2} dx∫−∞∞​e−cx2dx

After last step’s dark magic, this is refreshingly simple. All we have to do is change coordinates to y=cxy = \sqrt{c} xy=c​x:

∫−∞∞e−y2dx=∫−∞∞e−y2dyc\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^2} dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^2} \frac{dy}{\sqrt{c}}∫−∞∞​e−y2dx=∫−∞∞​e−y2c​dy​

Now we can just factor out the constant and use our previous result:

∫−∞∞e−cx2dx=1c∫−∞∞e−y2dy=πc\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-c x^2} dx = \frac{1}{\sqrt{c}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^2} dy = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{c}}∫−∞∞​e−cx2dx=c​1​∫−∞∞​e−y2dy=cπ​​

Nice.

Adding The Linear Term

We’re finally ready to tackle the full (functional) form of the integral:

∫−∞∞e−cx2+dxdx\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-c x^2 + d x} dx∫−∞∞​e−cx2+dxdx

This is not as complicated as it seems. Whenever we see something of the form −cx2+dx-c x^2 + d x−cx2+dx, we should immediately think about completing the square:

−cx2+dx=−c(x2−dcx)=−c((x−d2c)2−d24c2)=−c(x−d2c)2+d24c-c x^2 + d x = -c (x^2 - \frac{d}{c} x) = -c \left(\left(x - \frac{d}{2 c}\right)^2 - \frac{d^2}{4 c^2}\right) = -c \left(x - \frac{d}{2 c}\right)^2 + \frac{d^2}{4 c}−cx2+dx=−c(x2−cd​x)=−c((x−2cd​)2−4c2d2​)=−c(x−2cd​)2+4cd2​

Substituting this into our exponent, we get

∫−∞∞e−cx2+dxdx=∫−∞∞e−c(x−d2c)2+d24cdx\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-c x^2 + d x} dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-c \left(x - \frac{d}{2 c}\right)^2 + \frac{d^2}{4 c}} dx∫−∞∞​e−cx2+dxdx=∫−∞∞​e−c(x−2cd​)2+4cd2​dx

Now we see the magic of this approach. Using exponent properties, we can split this to a contant exponent and an exponent of the form e−cy2e^{-c y^2}e−cy2:

∫−∞∞e−c(x−d2c)2+d24cdx=∫−∞∞e−c(x−d2c)2ed24cdx=ed24c∫−∞∞e−c(x−d2c)2dx\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-c \left(x - \frac{d}{2 c}\right)^2 + \frac{d^2}{4 c}} dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-c \left(x - \frac{d}{2 c}\right)^2} e^{\frac{d^2}{4 c}} dx = e^{\frac{d^2}{4 c}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-c \left(x - \frac{d}{2 c}\right)^2} dx∫−∞∞​e−c(x−2cd​)2+4cd2​dx=∫−∞∞​e−c(x−2cd​)2e4cd2​dx=e4cd2​∫−∞∞​e−c(x−2cd​)2dx

This is now a simple change of variables away from our previous integral. Swapping to y=x−d2cy = x - \frac{d}{2 c}y=x−2cd​:

ed24c∫−∞∞e−c(x−d2c)2dx=ed24c∫−∞∞e−cy2dy=ed24cπce^{\frac{d^2}{4 c}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-c \left(x - \frac{d}{2 c}\right)^2} dx = e^{\frac{d^2}{4 c}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-c y^2} dy = e^{\frac{d^2}{4 c}} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{c}}e4cd2​∫−∞∞​e−c(x−2cd​)2dx=e4cd2​∫−∞∞​e−cy2dy=e4cd2​cπ​​

Putting It All Together

We’re finally ready to evaluate the probability density of our small path:

12π∫−∞∞eipn22mℏtNeipn(ya−yb)ℏdpn=eipn22mℏtN+ipn(ya−yb)ℏ\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{i \frac{p_n^2}{2 m \hbar} \frac{t}{N}} e^{i \frac{p_n (y_a - y_b)}{\hbar}} dp_n = e^{i \frac{p_n^2}{2 m \hbar} \frac{t}{N} + i \frac{p_n (y_a - y_b)}{\hbar}}2π1​∫−∞∞​ei2mℏpn2​​Nt​eiℏpn​(ya​−yb​)​dpn​=ei2mℏpn2​​Nt​+iℏpn​(ya​−yb​)​

Our solution is for integrals of the form

∫−∞∞e−cx2+dx\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{- c x^2 + d x}∫−∞∞​e−cx2+dx

The corresponding coefficients in our case are

c=−i2mℏtNd=iya−ybℏc = \frac{-i}{2 m \hbar} \frac{t}{N} \\ d = i \frac{y_a - y_b}{\hbar}c=2mℏ−i​Nt​d=iℏya​−yb​​

We will simplify a little by writing tN=δNt\frac{t}{N} = \delta_N tNt​=δN​t. Plugging and chugging, we find that

∫−∞∞e−cx2+dx=ed24cπc=e−im(yb−ya)22δNti2mℏπδNt\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{- c x^2 + d x} = e^{\frac{d^2}{4 c}} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{c}} = e^{-i m \frac{(y_b - y_a)^2}{2 \delta_N t}} \sqrt{\frac{i 2 m \hbar \pi}{\delta_N t}}∫−∞∞​e−cx2+dx=e4cd2​cπ​​=e−im2δN​t(yb​−ya​)2​δN​ti2mℏπ​​

Finally, multiplying through by the constant 12π\frac{1}{2 \pi}2π1​ gives us our final probability density:

12π∫eipn22mℏtNeipn(ya−yb)ℏdpn=e−im(yb−ya)22δNtimℏ2πδNt\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int e^{i \frac{p_n^2}{2 m \hbar} \frac{t}{N}} e^{i \frac{p_n (y_a - y_b)}{\hbar}} dp_n = e^{-i m \frac{(y_b - y_a)^2}{2 \delta_N t}} \sqrt{\frac{i m \hbar}{2 \pi \delta_N t}}2π1​∫ei2mℏpn2​​Nt​eiℏpn​(ya​−yb​)​dpn​=e−im2δN​t(yb​−ya​)2​2πδN​timℏ​​
