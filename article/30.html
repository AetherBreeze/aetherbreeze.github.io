The Residue Theorem

I will now skip about a 40 minute video’s worth of background on complex analysis. Specifically, this video by Mathemaniac. It’s the best expository of complex analysis and the residue theorem I’ve ever seen by a long shot. We’re going to proceed with the assumption that you understand the residue theorem, so if you don’t, please take some time to watch that video. Seriously, it’s half a college course in one free package.

Got it? Good. I will now assume that you understand the residue theorem and why it’s important for complex integrals.

Recall that we changed the path of our integral to dance around two singularities on the momentum axis. In doing so, we introduced two new singularities at

pt=±ω2−iϵp_t = \pm \sqrt{\omega^2 - i \epsilon}pt​=±ω2−iϵ​

We will eventually take the limit ϵ→0\epsilon \to 0ϵ→0. In that limit, the square root simplifies to its linear term:

pt=±(ω−iϵ)p_t = \pm (\omega - i \epsilon)pt​=±(ω−iϵ)

Thus we can factor the denominator, in the limit, to

pt2−ω2+iϵ→(pt−ω+iϵ)(pt+ω−iϵ)p_t^2 - \omega^2 + i \epsilon \to (p_t - \omega + i \epsilon)(p_t + \omega - i \epsilon)pt2​−ω2+iϵ→(pt​−ω+iϵ)(pt​+ω−iϵ)

Let’s quickly expand the right term and confirm this:

(pt−ω+iϵ)(pt+ω−iϵ)=pt2−ω2+2iωϵ+ϵ2(p_t - \omega + i \epsilon)(p_t + \omega - i \epsilon) = p_t^2 - \omega^2 + 2 i \omega \epsilon + \epsilon^2(pt​−ω+iϵ)(pt​+ω−iϵ)=pt2​−ω2+2iωϵ+ϵ2

In the limit, any finite multiple of ϵ\epsilonϵ is just ϵ\epsilonϵ, and terms of order ϵ2\epsilon^2ϵ2 vanish, so this reduces to

pt2−ω2+2iωϵ+ϵ2→pt2−ω2+iϵp_t^2 - \omega^2 + 2 i \omega \epsilon + \epsilon^2 \to p_t^2 - \omega^2 + i \epsilonpt2​−ω2+2iωϵ+ϵ2→pt2​−ω2+iϵ

as desired. Thus in the limit, our integral of interest factors out to

∫ei(y−x)tpt(pt−(ω−iϵ))(pt+(ω−iϵ))dpt\int \frac{e^{i (y - x)_t p_t}}{(p_t - (\omega - i \epsilon))(p_t + (\omega - i \epsilon))} dp_t∫(pt​−(ω−iϵ))(pt​+(ω−iϵ))ei(y−x)t​pt​​dpt​

Finding the Residues

Now that the integrand’s denominator is factored, it’s much easier to apply the residue theorem. There are two singularities to consider:

pt=±(ω−iϵ)p_t = \pm (\omega - i \epsilon)pt​=±(ω−iϵ)

It’s possible to consider them together, but practically, it’s easier to take them on one by one. Thus we will now split our analysis in two halves: one where ptp_tpt​ is positive, and one where ptp_tpt​ is negative. (Note that we are assuming ω=pλ2−m2\omega = \sqrt{p_\lambda^2 - m^2}ω=pλ2​−m2​ is positive).

When ptp_tpt​ is positive, the singularity is at ω−iϵ\omega - i \epsilonω−iϵ. The residue at this point is

R+=ei(y−x)t(ω−iϵ)((ω−iϵ)+(ω−iϵ))R_+ = \frac{e^{i (y - x)_t (\omega - i \epsilon)}}{((\omega - i \epsilon) + (\omega - i \epsilon))}R+​=((ω−iϵ)+(ω−iϵ))ei(y−x)t​(ω−iϵ)​

Dropping terms of order ϵ\epsilonϵ in the exponent and combining them in the denominator, this simplifies to

R+=ei(y−x)t(ω−iϵ)2ω−iϵR_+ = \frac{e^{i (y - x)_t (\omega - i \epsilon)}}{2 \omega - i \epsilon}R+​=2ω−iϵei(y−x)t​(ω−iϵ)​

Similarly, when ptp_tpt​ is negative, the singularity at ω+iϵ\omega + i \epsilonω+iϵ has residue

R−=e−i(y−x)t(ω−iϵ)−2ω+iϵR_- = \frac{e^{-i (y - x)_t (\omega - i \epsilon)}}{-2 \omega + i \epsilon}R−​=−2ω+iϵe−i(y−x)t​(ω−iϵ)​

The above value isn’t too hard to derive. It’s a good test of your complex analysis knowledge to do so yourself.

We can now apply the residue theorem. The integral over positive ptp_tpt​ is just

2πiR+=2πiei(y−x)t(ω−iϵ)2ω−iϵ2 \pi i R_+ = 2 \pi i \frac{e^{i (y - x)_t (\omega - i \epsilon)}}{2 \omega - i \epsilon}2πiR+​=2πi2ω−iϵei(y−x)t​(ω−iϵ)​

and the integral over negative ptp_tpt​ is

2πiR+=−2πie−i(y−x)t(ω−iϵ)−2ω+iϵ2 \pi i R_+ = -2 \pi i \frac{e^{-i (y - x)_t (\omega - i \epsilon)}}{-2 \omega + i \epsilon}2πiR+​=−2πi−2ω+iϵe−i(y−x)t​(ω−iϵ)​

We can combine these using the step function step(pt)\text{step}(p_t)step(pt​). The step function is a simple “utility” function defined to be 000 when xxx is negative and 111 when xxx is positive. (Observant readers might intuit that the step function is just the integral of the spike function!) With this trick, we can write our full integral in one, fairly simple piece:

∫ei(y−x)tptpt2−ω2+iϵdpt=2πi2ω−iϵ(ei(y−x)t(ω−iϵ)step(pt)+e−i(y−x)t(ω−iϵ)step(−pt))\int \frac{e^{i (y - x)_t p_t}}{p_t^2 - \omega^2 + i \epsilon} dp_t = \frac{2 \pi i}{2 \omega - i \epsilon} (e^{i (y - x)_t (\omega - i \epsilon)} \text{step}(p_t) + e^{-i (y - x)_t (\omega - i \epsilon)} \text{step}(-p_t))∫pt2​−ω2+iϵei(y−x)t​pt​​dpt​=2ω−iϵ2πi​(ei(y−x)t​(ω−iϵ)step(pt​)+e−i(y−x)t​(ω−iϵ)step(−pt​))

Finally we see the payout for our efforts – the denominator no longer has singularities in ptp_tpt​, and we can finally the limit ϵ→0\epsilon \to 0ϵ→0:

∫ei(y−x)tptpt2−ω2+iϵdpt=2πi2ω(ei(y−x)tωstep(pt)+e−i(y−x)tωstep(−pt))\int \frac{e^{i (y - x)_t p_t}}{p_t^2 - \omega^2 + i \epsilon} dp_t = \frac{2 \pi i}{2 \omega} (e^{i (y - x)_t \omega} \text{step}(p_t) + e^{-i (y - x)_t \omega} \text{step}(-p_t))∫pt2​−ω2+iϵei(y−x)t​pt​​dpt​=2ω2πi​(ei(y−x)t​ωstep(pt​)+e−i(y−x)t​ωstep(−pt​))

We don’t need to worry about the singularities anymore – we’ve taken care of them using residues, and the remaining integral path will steer well clear of them. Nice!
